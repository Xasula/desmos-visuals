Product Rule as Growing Area

Overview

Say we have a function f(t) = a(t)b(t). The derivative of this function is

f'(t) = a'(t)b(t) + a(t)b'(t)

This is called the Product Rule. At first, it can feel like this formula comes out of nowhere. There is a formal proof, but this visual focuses on the intuition behind why the rule must be true.

Think of a(t) as the width of a rectangle and b(t) as the height. Then the product a(t)b(t) is the area of that rectangle.

Change the value of t. As you change t, the values of a(t) and b(t) also change. As a result, the area f(t) = a(t)b(t) changes.

Now give t a tiny nudge, called dt. The width changes by da, and the height changes by db. So the area changes too.

The big question is:

Why is df/dt = (da/dt)b + a(db/dt)?

Before looking at the rate of change, focus on the actual change in area, df. The new added area is the difference between the original blue rectangle's area f(t) and the new rectangle's area f(t+dt):

df = f(t+dt) - f(t) = a(t+dt)b(t+dt) - a(t)b(t) = d(ab)

The visual below shows where the extra area comes from.

Imagine you increase the value of t by a very small amount h = dt. The additional area comes from the green, purple, and black rectangles.

Explaining the colors

The blue rectangle is the original area a(t)b(t). This is the value of f(t) before changing t.

The purple strip appears because the width changes by da. Its height is still approximately b(t), so its area is da · b(t).

The green strip appears because the height changes by db. Its width is still approximately a(t), so its area is a(t) · db.

The small black corner has area da · db. Since dt is infinitesimal, the black area is negligible compared to the purple or green strips. In the derivative, that corner disappears.

df = da · b + a · db + da · db

As dt → 0, the corner term da · db becomes negligible, leaving the product rule:

d(ab) = da · b + a · db
d(ab)/dt = da/dt · b + a · db/dt
(ab)' = a'b + ab'

What you should notice

The derivative of a product has two main terms because both factors can change. One term measures the effect of changing a(t) while keeping b(t) fixed. The other measures the effect of changing b(t) while keeping a(t) fixed.

Try changing...

  • t, to move to a different moment in time.
  • h, to make the change in t smaller or larger.
  • a(t) and b(t), to test different products.

Guided questions

  1. Which part of the picture represents the original product a(t)b(t)?
  2. Which added region comes from the change in a(t)?
  3. Which added region comes from the change in b(t)?
  4. What happens to the black corner as h gets smaller?
  5. Why does the product rule need two terms instead of just one?

Common misconception

A common mistake is thinking that the derivative of a product should be a'(t)b'(t). But the picture shows that the change in area does not come from multiplying the two changes together. It mostly comes from the two strips: da · b and a · db.
