Slicing an Infinite Double Cone with a Plane to Get Conic Sections

This visualization shows how conic sections arise from slicing an infinite double cone with a plane. When the slicing plane is horizontal, the intersection is a circle. When the plane is tilted moderately, it cuts only one nappe of the cone, producing an ellipse. At the critical angle where the plane is parallel to the side of the cone, the intersection becomes a parabola. When the plane is tilted even more steeply and intersects both nappes of the cone, the result is a hyperbola.

Degenerate conics appear when the slicing plane passes through the origin. In this visualization, this happens when x_shift = 0 and z_shift = 0. A circle or ellipse collapses to a single point at the origin, represented by x² + y² = 0. A parabola degenerates into a line, such as x² = 0y. A hyperbola degenerates into a pair of intersecting lines, such as x² - y² = 0.

For those of you with elite 603 ball knowledge, the eccentricity is e = cos(θ) / cos(β).
