Overview

The Big Idea

A conic section can be built from two competing distances: d1, the distance from a point to the directrix, and d2, the distance from the same point to the focus. The eccentricity e controls how these distances compare:

d2 = e d1

In the graph, the vertical orange lines represent all points that are d1 away from the directrix; the purple circle represents all points taht are d2 away from the focus (origin). As d1 increases, watch how the purple circle grows compared to the spacing of the orange lines. The higher the e, the faster d2 grows relative to d1.

When e < 1, the purple circle grows more slowly than the orange directrix-distance lines move outward. The left orange line eventually catches up to the left end of the purple circle, closing the blue curve. The points stay bounded and form an ellipse.

When e = 1, the purple circle and the orange lines move at the same pace. This means two things:

  1. The left orange line never catches up to the left edge of the purple circle, so the blue curve never closes back on itself.
  2. The right edge of the purple circle also never catches up to the right orange line, so no second branch appears on the right.

When e > 1, the purple circle grows faster than the orange directrix-distance lines move outward. It outruns the directrix distance. This means two things:

  1. The left orange line never catches up to the left edge of the purple circle, so the blue curve stays open.
  2. The right edge of the purple circle eventually catches up to the right orange line, creating a second blue branch on the right side.

What You Should Notice

Eccentricity is not just a number that labels the conic. It describes the “speed ratio” between the focus distance and the directrix distance. Ellipses, parabolas, and hyperbolas come from changing that one ratio.
